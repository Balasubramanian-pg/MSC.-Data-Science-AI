## 1 From Point Estimates to Statistical Uncertainty

Statistics exists because data is incomplete.

If we could measure every individual in a population, there would be no uncertainty. We would simply calculate the true population [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean):

$$  
\mu = [\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\sum X}{N}  
$$

where:

- $$\mu$$ = population [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean)
    
- $$N$$ = population size
    
- $$X$$ = each population observation
    

But in real-world problems, complete population data is usually impossible or impractical to obtain:

- surveying every voter
    
- testing every manufactured chip
    
- measuring every patient's blood pressure
    
- checking every transaction for fraud
    

Instead, we collect a sample.

From this sample, we calculate the sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean):

$$  
\bar{x} = [\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\sum x_i}{n}  
$$

where:

- $$\bar{x}$$ = sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean)
    
- $$n$$ = sample size
    
- $$x_i$$ = individual sample observations
    

The sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) acts as an estimator of the population [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean).

## 2 Why Point Estimates Are Fundamentally Incomplete

Suppose two analysts independently sample student marks from the same university.

- Analyst A gets: $$\bar{x} = 72$$
    
- Analyst B gets: $$\bar{x} = 76$$
    

Neither analyst is necessarily wrong.

This happens because of sampling variability.

Different samples produce different statistics.

The key insight is:

> A statistic is itself a random variable.

That means the sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) has its own probability distribution called the sampling distribution of the [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean).
## 3 Sampling Distribution of the [Mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean)

If we repeatedly draw samples of size $$n$$ and compute their means:

$$  
\bar{x}_1, \bar{x}_2, \bar{x}_3, \dots  
$$

these means form a distribution.

## [Mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) of the Sampling Distribution

$$  
E(\bar{x}) = \mu  
$$

The sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) is an unbiased estimator of the population [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean).
## Standard Deviation of the Sampling Distribution

$$  
\sigma_{\bar{x}} = [\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}  
$$

This quantity is called the [standard error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error) of the [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean).

\sigma_{\bar{x}} = [\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}

This [formula](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#formula) explains one of the deepest principles in statistics:

> Larger samples reduce uncertainty.

Not linearly, though.

To cut uncertainty in half, you must quadruple the sample size.

That square-root relationship is why massive increases in data often produce surprisingly modest increases in precision.

## 4 Central Limit Theorem: The Engine Behind Confidence Intervals

The entire framework of interval estimation depends heavily on the Central Limit Theorem (CLT).

The CLT states:

> For sufficiently large sample sizes, the sampling distribution of the sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) becomes approximately normal, regardless of the population distribution.

Formally:

$$  
\bar{x} \sim N\left(\mu, [\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)  
$$

for large $$n$$.

This is astonishingly powerful because it means:

- populations can be skewed
    
- heavy-tailed
    
- non-normal
    
- irregular
    

and yet sample means behave predictably.

Without the CLT, much of classical statistics collapses.

## 5 Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval converts uncertainty into a quantitative range.

General structure:

## $$  
\text{Confidence Interval}

\text{Point Estimate}  
\pm  
\text{Margin of Error}  
$$

or equivalently:

## $$  
\text{CI}

\text{Estimator}  
\pm  
(\text{Critical Value})  
\times  
(\text{[Standard Error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error)})  
$$

## 6 Understanding the Margin of Error

The margin of error determines interval width.

$$  
E = (\text{Critical Value})(\text{[Standard Error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error)})  
$$

E = (\text{Critical Value})(\text{[Standard Error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error)})

The margin of error grows when:

- variability increases
    
- confidence level increases
    
- sample size decreases
    

This creates the core statistical tradeoff:

|Goal|Effect|
|---|---|
|Higher confidence|Wider interval|
|More precision|Narrower interval|
|Small sample|Less precision|
|Large sample|More precision|

You cannot simultaneously maximize confidence and minimize interval width without increasing sample size.

## 7 Confidence Level and Significance Level

Suppose confidence level is 95%.

Then:

$$  
\alpha = 1 - 0.95 = 0.05  
$$

where:

- $$\alpha$$ = significance level
    
- total tail probability = $$0.05$$
    

For a two-sided interval:

$$  
[\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\alpha}{2} = 0.025  
$$

Meaning:

- 2.5% area in left tail
    
- 2.5% area in right tail
    

For the standard normal distribution:

$$  
Z_{0.025} = 1.96  
$$

Z_{0.025} = 1.96

Thus:

$$  
95%  
\text{ of the normal distribution lies between }  
-1.96  
\text{ and }  
+1.96  
$$

## 8 Z-Interval for the [Mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean)

When population standard deviation $$\sigma$$ is known:

## $$  
\text{CI}

\bar{x}  
\pm  
Z_{\alpha/2}  
[\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}  
$$

\text{CI}=\bar{x}\pm Z_{\alpha/2}[\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}

Conditions:

- random sample
    
- independent observations
    
- population normal OR large sample size
    
- population standard deviation known
    
## 9 [Example](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W07 - Multiple Regression/L0/Module%207%20Introduction%20-%20Multiple%20Linear%20Regression.md#example) of a Z-Interval

Suppose:

- $$\bar{x} = 75$$
    
- $$\sigma = 12$$
    
- $$n = 64$$
    
- confidence level = 95%
    

## Step 1: Compute [Standard Error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error)

$$  
SE = \frac{12}{\sqrt{64}} = \frac{12}{8} = 1.5  
$$
## Step 2: Critical Value

$$  
Z_{0.025} = 1.96  
$$

## Step 3: Margin of Error

$$  
E = 1.96(1.5) = 2.94  
$$

## Step 4: Confidence Interval

$$  
75 \pm 2.94  
$$

Final interval:

$$  
(72.06,\ 77.94)  
$$

Interpretation:

We use a procedure that captures the true [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) 95% of the time in repeated sampling.

## 10 Why the Z-Interval Is Rare in Practice

In real applications, population standard deviation $$\sigma$$ is almost never known.

This is not a minor issue.

Replacing $$\sigma$$ with $$s$$ fundamentally changes the uncertainty structure.

Once $$\sigma$$ becomes estimated:

$$  
\sigma \rightarrow s  
$$

additional variability enters the problem.

That variability must be modeled.

This leads to the Student’s [t-distribution](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#t-distribution).
## 11 The Student's [t-Distribution](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#t-distribution)

The [t-distribution](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#t-distribution) was developed by William Sealy Gosset while working at Guinness.

He published under the pseudonym "Student" because the company prohibited employees from publishing research.

The [t-distribution](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#t-distribution) is:

- symmetric
    
- bell-shaped
    
- centered at zero
    

but compared to the normal distribution:

- heavier tails
    
- more spread
    
- more conservative
    

Why heavier tails?

Because estimating $$\sigma$$ introduces uncertainty.

The [t-distribution](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#t-distribution) "admits ignorance" more honestly than the normal distribution.

## 12 Degrees of Freedom

For one-sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) estimation:

$$  
df = n - 1  
$$

df = n - 1

Degrees of freedom represent how much independent information remains after estimating parameters.

[Example](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W07 - Multiple Regression/L0/Module%207%20Introduction%20-%20Multiple%20Linear%20Regression.md#example):

If $$n = 5$$ and the sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) is fixed, once four values are chosen, the fifth is forced.

Only four observations vary freely.

Thus:

$$  
df = 5 - 1 = 4  
$$

## 13 T-Interval for the [Mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean)

When $$\sigma$$ is unknown:

## $$  
\text{CI}

\bar{x}  
\pm  
t_{\alpha/2,df}  
\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}  
$$

\text{CI}=\bar{x}\pm t_{\alpha/2,df}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}

This is the most common confidence interval used in introductory statistics.

## 14 [Example](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W07 - Multiple Regression/L0/Module%207%20Introduction%20-%20Multiple%20Linear%20Regression.md#example) of a T-Interval

Suppose:

- $$\bar{x} = 82$$
    
- $$s = 10$$
    
- $$n = 25$$
    
- confidence level = 95%
    

## Step 1: Degrees of Freedom

$$  
df = 25 - 1 = 24  
$$

## Step 2: Critical Value

From t-table:

$$  
t_{0.025,24} \approx 2.064  
$$

## Step 3: [Standard Error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error)

$$  
SE = \frac{10}{\sqrt{25}} = 2  
$$


## Step 4: Margin of Error

$$  
E = 2.064(2) = 4.128  
$$

## Step 5: Confidence Interval

$$  
82 \pm 4.128  
$$

Final interval:

$$  
(77.872,\ 86.128)  
$$

## 15 Why Small Samples Are Dangerous

Small samples create multiple problems simultaneously:

- unstable sample means
    
- unstable standard deviations
    
- sensitivity to outliers
    
- non-normality effects amplified
    

The [t-distribution](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#t-distribution) compensates partially through heavier tails.

But it does not magically fix poor sampling.

A confidence interval built on biased or non-random data is still unreliable.

Statistics cannot rescue fundamentally bad data collection.

## 16 Factors Affecting Interval Width

## 16.1 Sample Size

Larger $$n$$:

$$  
\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}  
\downarrow  
$$

Thus:

- smaller [standard error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error)
    
- narrower interval
    
- greater precision
    
## 16.2 Variability

Larger $$\sigma$$ or $$s$$:

- larger [standard error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error)
    
- wider interval
    

Noisy populations are harder to estimate precisely.

## 16.3 Confidence Level

Higher confidence:

- larger critical value
    
- wider interval
    

Approximate critical values:

|Confidence Level|Z Critical Value|
|---|---|
|90%|1.645|
|95%|1.96|
|99%|2.576|

## 17 One-Sided Confidence Intervals

Not all inference problems are symmetric.

Sometimes we only care about:

- lower bounds
    
- upper bounds
    

[Example](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W07 - Multiple Regression/L0/Module%207%20Introduction%20-%20Multiple%20Linear%20Regression.md#example):

- minimum battery life
    
- maximum defect rate
    
- minimum drug effectiveness
    

A lower confidence bound:

$$  
\bar{x} - t_{\alpha,df}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}  
$$

An upper confidence bound:

$$  
\bar{x} + t_{\alpha,df}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}  
$$

One-sided intervals place all significance level $$\alpha$$ into one tail.

## 18 Confidence Intervals vs Hypothesis Testing

These are deeply connected.

For a two-sided hypothesis test:

If the hypothesized parameter value lies outside the confidence interval:

$$  
H_0  
\text{ is rejected}  
$$

If it lies inside:

$$  
H_0  
\text{ is not rejected}  
$$

Confidence intervals often provide more information because they show:

- effect size
    
- uncertainty
    
- plausible parameter range
    

rather than merely "reject/not reject."

## 19 Frequentist Interpretation vs Bayesian Interpretation

Classical confidence intervals are frequentist.

The parameter is fixed.  
The interval is random.

Bayesian statistics flips this:

- interval fixed after observing data
    
- parameter treated probabilistically
    

A Bayesian credible interval _does_ allow statements like:

> "There is a 95% probability the parameter lies in this interval."

But that statement depends on prior assumptions.

Many beginners unintentionally interpret frequentist confidence intervals using Bayesian language.

This conceptual confusion is extremely common.

## 20 The Most Common Misinterpretations

## Incorrect

> "95% of data points lie in the interval."

Wrong.  
The interval concerns the population parameter, not individual observations.

## Incorrect

> "There is a 95% probability the parameter is inside."

Wrong under frequentist inference.

## Incorrect

> "A 99% interval is always better."

Not necessarily.

Higher confidence means less precision.

Overly wide intervals can become practically useless.

A confidence interval is fundamentally a statement about:

$$  
\text{[signal](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#[signal](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W07 - Multiple Regression/L2/Significance%20Testing%20and%20Multicollinearity.md#signal))}  
\quad vs \quad  
\text{[noise](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#[noise](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W07 - Multiple Regression/L2/Significance%20Testing%20and%20Multicollinearity.md#noise))}  
$$

The estimate:

$$  
\bar{x}  
$$

is the [signal](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#[signal](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W07 - Multiple Regression/L2/Significance%20Testing%20and%20Multicollinearity.md#signal)).

The interval quantifies how uncertain we are about separating the two.

## Conclusions

Confidence intervals move us from a single "best guess" to a range of plausible values, providing a measure of the uncertainty inherent in sampling.

### 1. Anatomy of a Confidence Interval

The structure of every two-sided confidence interval is:

$$\text{Confidence Interval} = \text{Point Estimate} \pm \text{Margin of Error}$$

- **Point Estimate:** The sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) ($\bar{x}$), acting as the center of the interval.
    
- **Margin of Error ($E$):** The "radius" of the interval, calculated as:
    
    $$\text{Margin of Error} = (\text{Critical Value}) \times (\text{[Standard Error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error)})$$
    
    - **[Standard Error](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#standard-error):** Measures the typical amount of error between the sample [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) ($\bar{x}$) and the population [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) ($\mu$).
        

### 2. Choosing the Correct Distribution

The choice of distribution depends on whether the population standard deviation ($\sigma$) is known.

|**Scenario**|**Known σ**|**Unknown σ**|
|---|---|---|
|**Distribution**|Standard Normal ($Z$)|Student's $t$|
|**[Formula](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W06 - Simple Linear Regression/L2/Testing%20for%20Significance%20in%20Regression.md#formula)**|$CI = \bar{x} \pm Z_{\alpha/2} [\frac{](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L1/Inferences%20for%20Two%20Population%20Means.md#frac)\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$|$CI = \bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2, df} \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$|
|**Notes**|Rare in practice|Uses sample std dev ($s$) and $df=n-1$|

### 3. Critical Interpretations & Constraints

Understanding the "Frequentist" nature of these intervals is vital to avoiding common traps:

- **The Misconception:** It is **incorrect** to say there is a 95% probability the true [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) ($\mu$) lies within your specific interval. The true [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) is a fixed constant; it is either inside or outside the interval (100% or 0% probability).
    
- **The Correct Interpretation:** We are 95% confident in the **method**. If we repeated this sampling process many times, we expect 95% of the resulting intervals to successfully capture the true population [mean](https://github.com/Balasubramanian-pg/MSC.-Data-Science-AI/blob/main/Trimester%201/Statistical%20Modelling%20%26%20Inferencing/W04 - Estimation And Hypothesis Testing Cont/L2/Testing%20Population%20Proportions.md#mean) ($\mu$).
    

> "Confidence is in the long-run success rate of the procedure, not in any single result".
