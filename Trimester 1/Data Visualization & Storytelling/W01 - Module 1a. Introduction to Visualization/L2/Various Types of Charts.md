# 1. Introduction to Chart Selection

## 1.1 Why Choosing the Right Chart Matters

The lecture focuses on a central principle of visualization:

> The effectiveness of a visualization depends heavily on selecting the correct chart type.

A visualization can fail even if the underlying data is correct because:

- the wrong chart obscures patterns
    
- comparisons become difficult
    
- relationships disappear
    
- audiences misinterpret trends
    

The lecture frames chart selection as a decision-making problem rather than a cosmetic one.

---

## 1.2 Visualization as Purpose-Driven Communication

The instructor repeatedly emphasizes:

```text
Every visualization exists to answer a specific analytical question.
```

The lecture categorizes visualizations into four major purposes:

|Purpose|Analytical Goal|
|---|---|
|Comparison|Compare categories|
|Distribution|Understand spread and clusters|
|Composition|Understand parts of a whole|
|Relationship|Explore connections between variables|

This framework is foundational in Business Intelligence and Analytics.

---

# 2. Understanding Variable Types Before Charting

Before selecting charts, it is critical to understand the underlying data structure.

## 2.1 Categorical Variables

**Categorical variables** represent groups or labels.

### Examples from Lecture

- Airline complaint categories
    
- Sentiment classes
    
- State names
    

### Suitable Charts

- Bar chart
    
- Pie chart
    
- Stacked bar chart
    

---

## 2.2 Numerical Variables

### Continuous Variables

Can take any value within a range.

#### Examples

- Weight of cars
    
- Height of people
    
- Voter turnout ratio
    

### Discrete Variables

Countable integers.

#### Examples

- Number of tweets
    
- Number of complaints
    
- Number of words
    

### Suitable Charts

- Histogram
    
- Scatter plot
    
- Bubble chart
    

---

## 2.3 Binary and Sentiment Variables

The lecture introduces sentiment categories:

- Positive
    
- Neutral
    
- Negative
    

These are effectively categorical sentiment buckets.

### Suitable Charts

- Bar chart
    
- Pie chart
    
- Bubble chart
    

---

## 2.4 Relationship Variables

Relationship charts require:

- two or more variables
    
- interactions
    
- correlations
    
- clustering patterns
    

### Examples

- Tweet sentiment vs issue category
    
- Word frequency vs sentiment
    
- Complaint count vs issue type

# 9. Visualization Decision Framework

## What Do You Want to Show?

```text
What is your analytical objective?
│
├── Compare categories?
│   ├── Few categories → Vertical Bar Chart
│   ├── Many categories → Horizontal Bar Chart
│   └── Compare composition simultaneously → Stacked Bar Chart
│
├── Show distribution?
│   ├── Frequency distribution → Histogram
│   ├── Outliers/clusters → Scatter Plot
│   └── Statistical spread → Box Plot
│
├── Show parts of a whole?
│   ├── Simple proportions → Pie Chart
│   └── Hierarchical composition → Stacked Bar
│
├── Explore relationships?
│   ├── Two variables → Scatter Plot
│   ├── Three variables → Bubble Chart
│   └── Text frequency → Word Cloud
│
├── Show trend over time?
│   └── Line Chart
│
└── Show geographic patterns?
    └── Maps / Choropleths
```

---

# 10. Golden Rules of Effective Chart Design

1. Always choose charts based on analytical purpose.
    
2. Comparison tasks are best handled by bar charts.
    
3. Distribution charts reveal averages, clusters, and spread.
    
4. Composition charts explain contribution to a whole.
    
5. Relationship charts uncover hidden patterns.
    
6. Horizontal bar charts work best for long category labels.
    
7. Ordered charts improve readability dramatically.
    
8. Histograms are critical for understanding operational ranges.
    
9. Bubble charts enable multi-dimensional analysis.
    
10. Word clouds simplify large-scale text analysis.
    
11. Use color intentionally to convey meaning.
    
12. Avoid clutter and unnecessary elements.
    
13. Label axes clearly and consistently.
    
14. Scale axes properly to avoid distortion.
    
15. Every visualization should answer a business question.
    
16. Visualization is about insight extraction, not decoration.
    
17. The best charts reduce cognitive effort.
    
18. Effective visuals support strategic decision-making.
    
19. Relationship analysis is essential for exploratory analytics.
    
20. Poor chart selection can completely hide important insights.
