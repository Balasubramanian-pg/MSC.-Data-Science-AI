Welcome to this module on feature engineering for text data. Before we  

get into specific techniques, let's start by understanding why feature  

engineering for text is fundamentally different from what we do with  

traditional structured data. In most machine learning tasks with tabular  

data, we deal with well-defined features like age, income, or product  

price. These are numerical or categorical, and we can directly apply  

techniques like normalisation, binning, or encoding, but text is messy. It's  

unstructured, highly variable, and context dependent. Words carry meaning,  

tone, and relationships, but none of this is obvious to a machine. Raw text  

in its original form cannot be processed directly by most algorithms.  

That's where feature engineering comes in. We need to convert text into a form  

that models can understand, typically numeric vectors, while preserving as  

much meaning as possible, and that's not a trivial task. Let's take an  

example. The sentences I love this movie and this movie is great are very  

similar in meaning. But a computer does not know that unless we represent them  

in a way that captures the underlying semantics. We need sophisticated  

methods that capture context, word order, and even grammar. In this module,  

we will explore exactly how that's done. We will cover both classic techniques  

like bag of words and TFIDF and modern approaches like word embeddings and  

part of speech tagging. You will learn how to transform raw text into features  

that actually help your models learn better. So whether you're building a  

sentiment classifier or a chatbot, this foundation will help you prepare your  

text data the right way.
